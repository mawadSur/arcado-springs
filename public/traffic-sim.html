Traffic Impact: Existing vs Proposed Conditions

Arcado Springs is a proposed walkable, mixed-use town center at 4541 Arcado Rd in Lilburn, GA. This simulation shows the idea in motion: when shops, dining, and homes share one site, many trips never become car trips, and an organized site entrance smooths what remains — so the same regional demand puts less pressure on the corridor.

What changes, in plain language

No engineering background needed — here is the idea, step by step.

THE ISSUE TODAY

Every errand is a drive

Arcado Rd is a two-lane collector funneling subdivision and school traffic to one signalized corner with Killian Hill Rd. In today's single-use pattern, almost every trip becomes a car trip out to that busy corner, and the school and commute peaks pile it all into a few tight hours.

WHAT CHANGES

Homes, shops, and dining on one walkable site

When destinations sit together, a real share of trips start and end on-site or on foot. The build-out also organizes site access: one coordinated driveway, a new signal timed with the main corner, a dedicated left-turn lane, and protected pedestrian crossings.

HOW THE PATTERN SHIFTS

Fewer cars, and the rest keep moving

Internal capture and walking remove trips at the source, so fewer cars ever reach the corner. The turn lane keeps the through lane clear, and the coordinated signal moves traffic in steady platoons instead of stop-and-go — so queues form later and clear faster.

WHAT PEOPLE NOTICE

A shorter line, a smoother corner

At the evening peak the difference is largest: average delay drops, the queue shortens, and the corner serves more cars each hour — lifting the level of service a grade or two. It complements the Killian Hill widening already underway rather than competing with it.

Methodology, data & honest limits

Read this first

This simulation is a communication tool built on an illustrative planning model, not a sealed traffic-impact study. The relationships it shows are grounded in published research, but the specific figures are modeled estimates and should be read as directional ranges, not guarantees or measured counts.

How the numbers are modeled

  • ITE-style trip generation sets the baseline external demand per scenario and scales it over the time window (peak-of-peak mid-window).
  • Mixed-use internal capture reduces external trips at the source — modeled at about 15–30% (roughly 30%+ at the PM peak), consistent with NCHRP Report 684.
  • A walk/bike mode shift removes additional short trips entirely (about 7–10% here).
  • Supply-side fixes — a new coordinated signal and a dedicated left-turn bay — raise saturation flow and cut spillback. Lower demand plus higher capacity yields lower delay, shorter queues, higher throughput, and a better LOS grade.

What a real study would add (Section 9 — data)

  • Measured turning-movement and 24-hour tube counts at Arcado Rd & Killian Hill Rd and the proposed driveway.
  • Approach-by-approach signal timing, phasing, and cycle data from Gwinnett County.
  • Site-specific ITE land-use codes, sizes, and pass-by / internal-capture surveys for the actual tenant mix.
  • Calibrated HCM or microsimulation (Synchro / VISSIM / SimTraffic) with 95th-percentile queue outputs.
  • Crash history, pedestrian counts, transit, and the committed Killian Hill widening as a background condition.

How it would be built for production (Section 10 — stack)

  • Demand & capacity from a calibrated HCM/Synchro model; queues and delay exported per scenario.
  • A microsimulation engine (VISSIM or open-source SUMO) for vehicle-level dynamics and signal coordination.
  • Geospatial basemap (this page uses Leaflet + a CARTO/OpenStreetMap light street basemap), with the real road network as projected lat/lng polylines.
  • A lightweight canvas overlay for the animated agents, decoupled from and re-projected against the locked basemap.
  • Outputs reviewed and sealed by a licensed traffic engineer before any official LOS determination.

Sources & honest caveats

  • ITE Trip Generation; NCHRP Report 684 (internal capture); the 2022 National Household Travel Survey; EPA Smart Growth; Ewing & Cervero (2010).
  • Results depend on the real land-use mix, true internal walkability, and connectivity — so they are ranges, not promises.
  • Map geometry is schematic and not to scale; agent counts are visual, not literal vehicle volumes.
  • This page does not constitute a traffic-impact study, an approval, or an official level-of-service finding.
